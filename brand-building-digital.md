# The Art of Brand Building in Digital-First Markets

**By Md. Shafin Shahriar Akhond** | Oct 2025 | 8 min read

*Brand equity used to be built over decades through mass media and retail presence. Digital has compressed that timeline and complicated it simultaneously. Brands can rise faster than ever — and collapse just as fast.*

---

There's a tension at the heart of brand building right now that doesn't get discussed enough. Digital channels offer brands unprecedented reach, personalization capability, and feedback speed. They also create an environment where brand consistency is harder to maintain, trust is harder to earn, and the noise floor is so high that standing out requires more creativity, more discipline, and more strategic clarity than ever before. Understanding that tension — and building strategy that accounts for it — is the core challenge of brand work in the 2020s.

I got an interesting vantage point on this during my time with the MAGGI brand team at Nestlé Bangladesh. MAGGI is one of the most established brands in the market — decades of equity, massive distribution, high recall. Yet even with all that heritage, the team was constantly grappling with how to maintain relevance in an environment where the consumer's digital attention had fragmented in ways that legacy media planning simply couldn't fully address. Watching a strong brand navigate that challenge taught me more about digital brand management than any course I've taken.

## Purpose-Driven Branding Is Not Optional Anymore

81% of consumers say trust is a prerequisite before they'll make a purchase. That's not a marginal finding — it's a fundamental shift in the purchase dynamic. Brands can no longer operate as if the transaction is the relationship. Consumers, especially younger ones, evaluate brands on values, behavior, and consistency long before they evaluate product features or price. The brand decision often happens before the category need even arises.

Purpose-driven branding is the response to this reality — but not "purpose" as a campaign theme where a brand associates itself with a social cause for a few weeks and then reverts to transactional messaging. That version of purpose is increasingly recognized for what it is, and the backlash when it's exposed as performative can be severe and lasting. Genuine purpose branding is when the values a brand articulates are visibly embedded in its actual decisions: sourcing, pricing, community investment, how it handles a crisis.

For FMCG brands specifically, this plays out in concrete ways. Transparency about ingredients and production, commitment to nutrition, environmental impact of packaging — these are no longer niche concerns for specialized consumer segments. They're mainstream expectations, increasingly so in markets like Bangladesh where a mobile-connected younger generation has the same access to global brand criticism and accountability journalism as consumers in any developed market. The information asymmetry that once protected brands from scrutiny is largely gone.

## Digital-First Brand Identity

A brand identity built for television and print doesn't automatically translate to digital contexts. This sounds obvious, but the execution failures are everywhere. Logos that don't read at 32×32 pixels. Color systems that become indistinguishable on mobile screens in bright sunlight. Tone-of-voice guidelines developed for formal brand communications that collapse when a social media manager is trying to respond to a consumer comment in real time under time pressure.

Building a digital-first brand identity means designing for the actual contexts where your consumer encounters the brand: a phone screen, a 15-second video, a push notification, a search result snippet. The visual and verbal elements need to be modular enough to work at different scales and speeds without losing coherence. Strong brands in digital-first environments tend to have simpler, bolder visual systems — not because simplicity is inherently better, but because complexity degrades faster across formats and the simpler system retains more brand recognition across more contexts.

The tone of voice question is particularly interesting and underinvested by most brand teams. Brands that feel authentic in digital spaces tend to have voices that can flex between formal and conversational without feeling inconsistent — a brand that publishes a carefully crafted thought leadership post can also respond to a consumer tweet with warmth and personality, and both feel like the same entity. That range requires intentional design and real examples in the brand guidelines, not just a list of adjectives.

## Social Commerce and the Collapsing Purchase Funnel

One of the most significant structural shifts in FMCG marketing over the last three years is the rise of social commerce — the integration of discovery, consideration, and purchase within a single social platform experience. In markets across Southeast Asia, this is already the dominant e-commerce paradigm for many categories. TikTok Shop, Instagram Shopping, and platform-specific live commerce formats have collapsed the traditional funnel into a single continuous session.

I watched this shift play out with MAGGI's social presence. The challenge isn't reach — a large brand with paid media budget can generate impressions efficiently. The challenge is making content that converts within the platform context rather than redirecting users elsewhere and losing most of them in the friction of that transition. Native, platform-appropriate content that drives purchase within the session is a fundamentally different creative and strategic brief than a traditional digital advertisement.

For brands navigating this shift, creator partnerships are increasingly the mechanism that makes social commerce work at scale. Creators have already built the trust and native context within a platform community that brands have to work hard to earn. A creator who genuinely uses and recommends a product provides social proof in a form that no brand-produced content can replicate, however well-crafted. The key word is genuinely — audiences have developed sophisticated instincts for sponsored content that doesn't reflect actual usage, and inauthenticity in creator partnerships is a trust liability that can cost more than the partnership gained.

## Hyper-Personalization at Scale

Digital channels make personalization possible in ways that were simply unimaginable in a mass media environment. This creates an opportunity and a responsibility simultaneously. Consumers expect relevance — they've been conditioned by years of algorithmic content to receive things that feel appropriate for them specifically. Brand communications that feel generic are penalized with lower engagement, higher skip rates, and opt-out behavior that's difficult to reverse.

Effective personalization in marketing isn't about using someone's first name in an email subject line. It's about delivering content, offers, and messages that are genuinely relevant to where that consumer is in their relationship with the brand, what they've previously engaged with, and what they demonstrably care about. That requires both a robust data infrastructure and creative flexibility — the ability to produce enough variations of content to actually differentiate the experience meaningfully across consumer segments.

> The brand that wins isn't the one with the biggest budget or the most data. It's the one that uses both to feel genuinely human in every interaction — relevant, trustworthy, and consistent.

## Omnichannel Consistency: The Non-Negotiable

With consumers moving between touchpoints constantly — social media, search, e-commerce, physical retail, peer recommendation — brand experience needs to be consistent in ways it never had to be before. A consumer who sees a brand on Instagram, researches it on Google, and then encounters it in-store should feel like they're interacting with the same entity at each point. Inconsistency across these touchpoints erodes trust in ways that are difficult to diagnose because the consumer rarely articulates it explicitly — they just feel less confident about the brand and the purchase feels riskier.

Building genuine omnichannel consistency requires organizational alignment, not just design standards. The marketing team, the digital team, the sales team, and the retail team all need to be operating from the same brand story and the same consumer understanding. When those teams work in silos — which is the default at most organizations regardless of how the org chart is drawn — inconsistency is the natural outcome.

The brands navigating this best tend to treat brand consistency as a governance challenge as much as a creative one. It's not enough to have a brand book that lives on a shared drive nobody opens. You need processes, accountability, and genuine leadership commitment to the idea that every consumer touchpoint reflects the same brand. That's harder than it sounds in practice, especially as digital channels multiply and ownership fragments across teams and agency partners. But it's where the real, enduring work of brand building in the digital age actually gets done.

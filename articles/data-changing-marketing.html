How Data is Changing Marketing Decision Making

By Md. Shafin Shahriar Akhond · Apr 2026 · 7 min read

I'll be honest: when I first joined the MAGGI brand team at Nestlé Bangladesh, I thought marketing was mostly about creativity. You develop a strong campaign idea, nail the messaging, launch it, and hope it resonates. What I didn't expect was how much of that process would be governed by numbers — brand health indices, awareness scores, distribution metrics, share-of-voice data. By the end of my internship, I was building trackers in Excel that the team relied on weekly. That experience changed how I think about marketing entirely.

The shift toward data-driven marketing isn't some distant industry trend. It's already happened. The question now is whether individual marketers are keeping up — and whether the organizations they work for have built the culture and infrastructure to make data genuinely actionable rather than just decorative.

The Numbers Don't Lie — And Neither Do Marketers Who Use Them

A few statistics that stuck with me: 83% of marketers now say data-driven marketing is essential to their work. Companies with strong data cultures make decisions five times faster than their peers. And perhaps most striking — data-driven organizations are 23 times more likely to acquire customers than those still relying on intuition alone. These aren't minor advantages. In a competitive market, that kind of gap compounds fast.

The thing is, most marketers already believe in data. The divide isn't between believers and skeptics — it's between those who know how to actually operationalize data and those who collect it but can't do much with it. I've seen brand teams drown in dashboards that nobody reads because nobody agreed on what they were supposed to answer. The volume of data available has outpaced most teams' ability to use it meaningfully, and that gap doesn't close on its own.

From Campaign Gut-Feel to KPI Frameworks

At Nestlé, tracking MAGGI's brand health wasn't about vanity metrics. The team cared about specific, actionable indicators — top-of-mind awareness, trial rates, brand affinity scores among specific demographic cohorts. These KPIs weren't chosen randomly. They traced back directly to business questions: Are we winning in the right consumer segments? Is our advertising actually shifting purchase intent or just generating impressions?

That's the discipline most marketing teams lack. They track what's easy to measure — clicks, reach, follower counts — without asking whether those metrics actually map to the outcomes the business cares about. Data-driven marketing begins with the question, not the metric. You don't build a dashboard and then figure out what it's for. You decide what decision it needs to support and work backwards.

Campaign KPI tracking forced me to think like a strategist. Every week, I'd look at where numbers were moving and ask why. A spike in social mentions — was it organic conversation or a PR moment? A dip in brand recall — was it media weight, message fatigue, or competitive noise? The data never gave you the answer outright. It gave you the right question to dig into. That's actually a skill in itself: reading movement in data and knowing what kind of follow-up investigation it warrants.

The Democratization Problem

One thing nobody talks about enough: data is only transformative when it's accessible. At many organizations — especially in South Asia — data lives in silos. The research team has one set of numbers, finance has another, the brand team has their own tracker, and rarely do these three sit in the same room and align.

This is partly a tools problem. Excel spreadsheets passed around over email aren't a data infrastructure. But it's also a culture problem. When data literacy is concentrated among a few analysts, everyone else either waits for insights or makes decisions without them. Neither is a good outcome. The bottleneck isn't data collection — it's data activation.

The most data-mature organizations I've read about — and the ones that show up consistently in McKinsey and Gartner research on this topic — treat data literacy as a core competency for every function, not just analytics teams. Marketing professionals who can read a regression output, understand statistical significance, or build their own pivot table analysis are genuinely more effective than those who can't. That's not a nice-to-have anymore. It's increasingly a baseline expectation for anyone who wants to operate at the strategy level.

Real-Time Data and the Speed Advantage

One of the most meaningful shifts in the last five years has been the move from monthly reporting cycles to real-time or near-real-time visibility. When I was building trackers at Nestlé, data was still largely backward-looking — you'd review last week's numbers to inform next week's decisions. Modern tools have compressed that loop dramatically.

Social listening platforms, programmatic ad dashboards, and e-commerce analytics can now surface consumer signals within hours of a campaign going live. For FMCG brands operating in volatile markets — where price sensitivity is high and competitive activity is constant — that speed is a genuine competitive edge. You can catch a messaging problem in day two of a campaign instead of discovering it in the post-mortem three weeks later. The difference between a course correction and a failed campaign is often just how quickly you identified the signal.

The best marketing teams aren't the ones with the most data. They're the ones who've decided exactly which data matters and built the discipline to act on it quickly.

What This Means for Marketers at Every Level

If you're early in your career — which I am — the temptation is to position yourself as a creative or a strategist and leave the data stuff to analysts. I'd push back on that instinct hard. The marketers I've observed who command the most credibility in a room are the ones who can tell a story with numbers. Not because they're statisticians, but because they've trained themselves to think in terms of evidence.

That means building practical skills: Excel for data organization and analysis, Power BI or Tableau for visualization, a working understanding of how to design and read A/B tests, and enough Google Analytics or Meta Ads Manager literacy to interrogate campaign performance yourself. None of this is especially hard. It's mostly about discipline and curiosity. Most of what you need can be learned through focused practice over a few months, not years of formal training.

I'm pursuing a dual master's in Commerce and Marketing at Macquarie University precisely because I want to build that quantitative foundation without losing the creative and strategic instincts that drew me to marketing in the first place. The future belongs to marketers who can do both — who can move from a regression model to a campaign concept in the same afternoon without either discipline feeling foreign.

The Human Element Still Matters

None of this is an argument that creativity is dead or that intuition has no place in marketing. It absolutely does. Data tells you what's happening and often why. It rarely tells you what to do next. That leap — from insight to idea — still requires human judgment, cultural fluency, and creative vision. The most beautifully optimized campaign brief still needs a great idea to become a great campaign.

What data does is narrow the field. It eliminates the obviously wrong paths, highlights the opportunities worth pursuing, and gives your creative instincts a more accurate map to work with. The best marketers I've encountered treat data not as a constraint but as a creative input — raw material for better thinking.

That's the mental model I'm working toward. Not data instead of intuition, but data informing it. The shift is subtle but it changes everything about how you approach a brief, a campaign, or a brand challenge. When you've done it enough, you stop thinking of it as a shift at all — it just becomes how you think.

# FMCG Consumer Behavior in Emerging Markets

**By Md. Shafin Shahriar Akhond** | Mar 2026 | 8 min read

*Selling to consumers in South and Southeast Asia isn't just a scaled-down version of selling in developed markets. The rules are different, the motivations are layered, and brand strategies that win in London or Sydney often fall flat in Dhaka or Jakarta.*

---

Working on the MAGGI brand in Bangladesh gave me a front-row seat to something that most marketing textbooks treat as a footnote: the sheer complexity of the emerging market consumer. MAGGI isn't just a noodle brand here. For many households, it's a trusted staple, an aspirational product, and a familiar comfort all at once. Understanding how those layers interact is the real work of FMCG brand management in this part of the world.

Southeast Asia's FMCG sector grew over 4% in 2025, powered by rising middle-class incomes, expanding urban populations, and a mobile-first retail shift. But growth at the macro level can mask wildly different realities at the consumer level. Price sensitivity, cultural context, and aspirational consumption don't move in sync — and brands that treat them as if they do tend to get humbled quickly. I watched this play out firsthand, and it fundamentally shaped how I think about brand strategy.

## Price Sensitivity Isn't What You Think It Is

The conventional assumption about emerging market consumers is simple: they're price sensitive, so lead with value. Cheaper products win. That's not wrong exactly, but it's dangerously incomplete.

Yes, 42% of consumers in these markets actively seek out lower-priced alternatives to the products they prefer. But notice the framing — alternatives to products they prefer. They're not abandoning brand loyalty; they're looking for ways to maintain it at a sustainable price point. This is a crucial distinction. The consumer hasn't stopped wanting MAGGI. They're asking whether they can still afford it this month, and what happens if they can't.

The strategic response to this isn't to discount your flagship product into irrelevance. It's to build a price-pack architecture that keeps your brand within reach across income levels. Smaller pack sizes, entry-price SKUs, and combo promotions aren't margin sacrifices — they're brand retention strategies. MAGGI in Bangladesh has approached this thoughtfully, maintaining premium positioning while offering accessible formats that keep the brand on the shopping list even in tighter months. The brand doesn't chase down-market; it builds a ladder so consumers can stay close regardless of the economic moment.

## Cultural Context Is the Invisible Competitor

Every FMCG brand operating in South Asia competes not just against other brands but against deeply embedded cultural habits. Food is the most obvious example. Bangladeshi consumers have extraordinarily strong food culture — specific tastes, preparation rituals, and meal occasions that have existed for generations. An instant noodle brand entering this market isn't just selling convenience; it's negotiating a relationship with tradition.

MAGGI navigated this brilliantly at a global level by localizing flavor profiles and positioning itself as a recipe ingredient rather than a standalone meal. That framing matters. "Cook with MAGGI" is a fundamentally different value proposition than "eat MAGGI instead of cooking." One respects the consumer's food culture; the other challenges it. In emerging markets, the brands that respect cultural context almost always outperform those that try to replace it.

This is also why local brands command 50%+ market share across many FMCG categories in South and Southeast Asia. They don't need to localize — they are local. They understand the idiom, the occasion, the family structure, the flavor preference at a granular level that no global brand team can manufacture from a regional office. Multinationals entering these markets need to earn that kind of credibility. It takes time, genuine consumer empathy, and the organizational humility to adapt rather than impose.

## Aspirational Consumption: The Layer Everyone Underestimates

Here's something that surprised me during my time at Nestlé: consumers who are highly price-sensitive in one category will actively choose premium products in another. A family stretching their grocery budget will still buy a branded biscuit over a private-label one if they associate that brand with quality or status. Aspirational consumption isn't reserved for luxury goods — it exists at every income level, just applied to different categories depending on what signals social participation and discernment in that specific cultural context.

In emerging markets, buying a recognized global brand carries social meaning. It signals access, discernment, and a certain kind of participation in a wider consumer culture. Brands that understand this can charge a premium that logic alone wouldn't justify. But they have to earn it consistently — through product quality, visible marketing presence, and the kind of word-of-mouth that comes when a product actually delivers on its promise.

> In emerging markets, the consumer's relationship with a brand is rarely just transactional. It's emotional, social, and sometimes aspirational. The brands that succeed long-term are the ones that take that seriously and build for it deliberately.

## Digital Is Reshaping the Purchase Journey — Unevenly

Mobile penetration has fundamentally changed how FMCG brands reach consumers in emerging markets. In Bangladesh, Facebook is the dominant discovery platform for a large portion of the consumer market. Brands that once relied exclusively on TV and in-store activations now need to maintain credible, active social presences to stay relevant. This is especially true for reaching younger consumers who are first-generation digital natives and whose primary brand encounters happen on a phone screen.

But digital isn't uniform across these markets. Rural penetration is lower. E-commerce adoption varies dramatically between urban centers and secondary cities. In-store and traditional trade channels — the kirana stores, the neighborhood shops, the market stalls — still account for the majority of FMCG volume in most of South Asia. A brand strategy that goes all-in on digital and forgets traditional distribution is leaving significant volume on the table.

The most effective approaches I've seen integrate both: digital for awareness, engagement, and brand-building; traditional trade for distribution, placement, and trial. These aren't competing channels — they're different moments in the same consumer journey. A consumer might discover a product through a Facebook ad, decide to try it based on recommendations from family, and then find it at the local store because the brand's distribution team made sure it was there. Each touchpoint contributed. None of them alone would have been sufficient.

## What Brand Teams Get Wrong

The biggest mistake global FMCG brand teams make in emerging markets is applying a template. They take a campaign that worked in Malaysia and deploy it in Bangladesh with the briefing modified, the visuals slightly adapted, and the media mix roughly unchanged. Sometimes it works. Often it doesn't, and when it doesn't, the post-mortem is usually some variation of "the market wasn't ready."

The market is usually ready. The campaign wasn't right for it. There's an important difference. Genuine market readiness for adaptation — the willingness to rebuild a strategy from consumer insight up rather than from a global template down — is what separates the brands that become household names in emerging markets from those that remain perpetual challengers.

I think about this a lot as I prepare for graduate studies focused on international marketing. The Asia-Pacific region is not a monolith. Bangladesh is not Indonesia. Indonesia is not Vietnam. Each market has its own consumer psychology, cultural gravity, and competitive dynamics. The skill that will matter most for the next generation of FMCG marketers isn't campaign execution — it's the ability to read a market on its own terms and build strategy that earns genuine resonance rather than just generating awareness.

## Looking Forward

The FMCG landscape in South and Southeast Asia is still in the middle of a massive structural shift. Rising incomes, digital adoption, urbanization, and changing household compositions are all reshaping what consumers want and how they shop. Brands that succeed in the next decade will be the ones that stay genuinely close to their consumers — investing in insight, not just in media weight — and that build strategies flexible enough to evolve as the market does.

For me, this space remains the most interesting and complex arena in marketing. It's where real brand-building happens — not in campaign concepts or social strategy decks, but in the daily choices that determine whether a brand earns a place in someone's household and keeps it. That challenge is what drew me to marketing in the first place, and it's what I intend to spend my career on.

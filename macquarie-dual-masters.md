# Why I Chose Dual Master's at Macquarie

**By Md. Shafin Shahriar Akhond** | Nov 2025 | 8 min read

*Choosing a graduate program is one of those decisions that's easy to overcomplicate and easy to get wrong. Here's how I thought about it — and why Macquarie University's dual Master's in Commerce and Marketing ended up being the clearest choice I could make.*

---

I spent a long time wrestling with the question of where to do my master's. Not in an anxious way, but in the way that happens when you care enough about the decision to sit with it for a while and really examine what you're actually trying to accomplish. I had a reasonably clear picture of where I wanted to go professionally — international marketing strategy, brand consulting, working across markets rather than within a single one. What I needed was a program that would actually build toward that, not just credential it.

I'm starting a dual Master's in Commerce and Marketing at Macquarie University in Sydney in July 2026. This is what I thought about when I made that choice, and what I believe the next two years will give me that I couldn't get any other way.

## The Strategic Logic of Doing Two Things at Once

The case for a dual degree isn't obvious at first. Most people hear it and think it just means more time in school. That's partly true. But the rationale for combining Commerce and Marketing specifically is something I thought about carefully over several months of reflection and research.

Marketing, at the senior level, is fundamentally a commercial function. The decisions that matter — portfolio strategy, market entry, pricing architecture, investment allocation — require you to understand how business models work, how value gets created and captured, and how financial performance maps back to brand decisions. Marketers who lack that commercial grounding are ceiling-limited. They can execute brilliantly and manage campaigns with skill, but they struggle to lead at the level where the real strategic choices happen. I've seen this dynamic play out clearly in my internship experience.

The Commerce degree gives me that foundation systematically — economics, finance, strategy, organizational behavior. The Marketing degree builds the domain expertise on top of it. Together, they're not two separate credentials sitting side by side; they're one integrated skillset. That integration is what I actually need to do the work I want to do. Choosing one over the other would have been a compromise I'd spend years compensating for.

## Why Sydney, Specifically

I could have looked at programs in the UK or North America. I didn't spend long on that path. Sydney made sense for a specific and considered reason: it's one of the most strategically positioned cities in the world for someone who wants to work across the Asia-Pacific region.

Australia sits at the intersection of Western business practice and Asian markets in a way that no European or North American city can replicate. Sydney is a hub for multinational brand operations in the APAC region — consumer goods companies, financial services firms, and consulting practices run significant regional operations from there. The professional ecosystem is genuinely oriented outward toward Asia, which is exactly the orientation I want to develop.

There's also something specific about Sydney's multicultural consumer base that's instructive for marketing. The city's population is genuinely varied in ways that are a live case study in managing diversity in brand strategy — not diversity as a theme, but as an operational reality that shapes how you write copy, design creative, and think about audience segmentation. Coming from Bangladesh, I've developed a deep understanding of one market. Sydney will give me exposure to the complexity of serving many simultaneously.

## What Macquarie Specifically Offers

I researched multiple universities and programs thoroughly before landing on Macquarie. A few things stood out. The Business School has a consistent emphasis on applied learning — this isn't primarily a theoretical program that keeps you at arm's length from real business problems. The curriculum integrates case-based learning and industry engagement in ways that matter for someone coming in with professional experience and wanting to develop it further rather than set it aside for two years.

The marketing discipline at Macquarie is particularly strong in digital marketing, consumer psychology, and marketing analytics — three areas where I want depth, not just familiarity. Given that I've already built some practical competency in these areas through my internships at Nestlé, Remark HB, and Startup Bangladesh, I wanted a program that would challenge me academically and push my thinking rather than simply confirm what I already knew.

The dual degree structure is also well-integrated — it's not two programs run in parallel with no connection between them. There's deliberate curriculum design that recognizes the relationship between the two disciplines and builds on it. That integration matters because it's precisely the connective tissue between commercial and marketing thinking where the most interesting problems live, and where I expect to grow the most.

## What I'm Hoping to Build

Graduate school, for me, isn't the destination — it's infrastructure for what comes after. I have a relatively clear sense of what I want to build toward: roles in international marketing strategy, ideally in the FMCG or brand consulting space, working across multiple markets in the Asia-Pacific region. That's specific enough to give direction without being so narrow that I'd rule out adjacent opportunities that emerge in ways I can't currently anticipate.

My internship experience has given me more confidence in that direction than I expected. Working on the MAGGI brand team at Nestlé showed me that the work I find most energizing is at the intersection of brand strategy and commercial outcomes — figuring out not just what a brand should stand for but how that positioning translates into market share, distribution decisions, and measurable consumer behavior change. At Remark HB, I got to see brand consulting from the agency side, which gave me a different vantage point on how brands are built and maintained over time.

> The version of my career I'm working toward requires both the analytical rigor of commerce and the strategic intuition of marketing. I don't want to choose between them. The dual degree is how I don't have to.

## A Note on the Personal Dimension

Every big decision has a personal dimension that doesn't show up cleanly in the rational argument. Mine is this: I've spent my whole life in Bangladesh. I love that context deeply, and I intend to bring everything I learn back into it in some form — whether that's working for multinationals with presence in Bangladesh, contributing to the growth of local brands, or eventually building something of my own. But there's a version of growth that only happens when you remove yourself from familiar context.

When you have to rebuild a professional identity in a new place, navigate systems you don't know by instinct, form relationships across cultural distances, and learn from people whose experiences are genuinely different from yours — that kind of growth compounds in ways that staying comfortable doesn't. Sydney will be that kind of challenge. I'm going into it clear-eyed about the difficulty and genuinely excited about it.

The best professional development I've experienced wasn't comfortable. My internship at Nestlé pushed me. Leading the Finance Society pushed me in different ways. Why would graduate school be different? The discomfort is part of the point. It's where the real work happens.

## What I'll Carry Forward

I'm under no illusion that a master's degree guarantees anything. What it does — if you approach it seriously — is create a concentrated window of structured learning and reflection that's hard to replicate while working full-time. Two years of sustained intellectual engagement with commerce and marketing, surrounded by people from different countries and professional backgrounds, will change how I think in ways I can't fully predict right now. That unpredictability is part of what I'm looking forward to.

What I'll carry into it is everything I've learned so far — from the MAGGI brand team, from running the Finance Society, from the brand consulting work at Remark HB, from the startup exposure at Startup Bangladesh. Graduate school works best when you bring something to it. I intend to. And I intend to bring what it gives me back to the markets and problems I care most about: brand building in South Asia and the broader Asia-Pacific region, where the stakes are high and the opportunities are genuinely extraordinary.

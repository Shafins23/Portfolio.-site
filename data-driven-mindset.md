# Building a Data-Driven Marketing Mindset

**By Md. Shafin Shahriar Akhond** | Dec 2025 | 7 min read

*The hardest part of becoming data-driven isn't learning the tools. It's unlearning the instinct to lead with your conclusion and find data to support it. That's a cultural shift, not a technical one.*

---

I went into my Nestlé internship thinking I was pretty analytically minded. I'd done quantitative coursework at BUP, I was comfortable in Excel, and I understood the basics of marketing metrics. What I didn't realize was how different it is to actually use data to guide decisions versus to report on outcomes after the fact. The gap between those two modes of thinking is where most marketing professionals quietly live — and where real growth happens when you cross it.

The shift from gut-feel marketing to evidence-based strategy isn't really about tools or dashboards. It's about the questions you ask at the beginning of a project rather than after it. That reorientation sounds simple. It takes real discipline to maintain, especially in fast-moving environments where the pressure to look decisive and move quickly often overrides the urge to actually understand what you're looking at before acting on it.

## Start With the Business Question

The most common analytical mistake I've observed — in myself and in others — is starting with metrics. We look at what data we have, we build reports around it, and we call that analysis. It's not. It's description. Real analysis starts with a question: Why did brand consideration drop in Q3? Which consumer segment is most likely to convert from trial to repeat purchase? Is our campaign driving incrementality or capturing demand that would have converted anyway?

Questions like these are uncomfortable because they might not have clean answers. They require you to design the right measurement approach before you collect data, which takes time and involves genuine intellectual discomfort. But the alternative — collecting everything and hoping meaning emerges — produces dashboards full of numbers that nobody can act on. It's the appearance of rigor without the substance of it.

At MAGGI, the brand health tracking process worked because it was grounded in specific business questions from the outset. What's our top-of-mind awareness trend among the 18-35 segment? How does purchase intent correlate with our above-the-line media spend? Those questions dictated which metrics mattered and which were noise. Everything else became background. The discipline of starting with the question — before touching the data — is the single most important habit a data-driven marketer can build.

## Choosing the Right KPIs — And Not Too Many

A practical principle I've internalized: focus your tracking on 10 to 20 key dimensions at most. More than that and you're no longer managing metrics — you're managing a metrics management problem. When everything is a KPI, nothing is. The paradox of measurement is that adding more metrics often produces less clarity, not more, because the signal gets buried in the noise of things you're watching that don't actually move.

Good KPIs share a few characteristics: they're directly tied to a business outcome, they're measurable consistently over time, they move when your actions change, and they're understandable by the people who need to act on them. That last point is often neglected. A KPI that requires three paragraphs of explanation before anyone can interpret it isn't useful in a decision-making context regardless of how technically rigorous it is.

I'd also distinguish between lagging indicators — outcomes you've already achieved, like sales revenue or market share — and leading indicators — signals that predict future outcomes, like brand consideration or share of search. A data-driven marketing team tracks both. Lagging indicators tell you how you did; leading indicators tell you where you're headed. Relying only on lagging indicators means you're always reacting to the past rather than managing toward the future.

## Building an A/B Testing Culture

One of the most tangible expressions of a data-driven mindset is the willingness to test before scaling. A/B testing — or more broadly, controlled experimentation — is the mechanism that separates "we think this will work" from "we know this worked under these conditions." It replaces the confidence of opinion with the confidence of evidence, and that's a fundamentally different kind of confidence.

Most marketing teams don't test consistently. They test opportunistically — when someone suggests it, when a vendor offers a split-run option, when there's time in the timeline. A real testing culture treats experimentation as the default, not the exception. It means having a backlog of hypotheses, a clear process for test design and analysis, and the organizational patience to wait for statistically meaningful results before drawing conclusions you'll stake decisions on.

The cultural prerequisite for this is treating failed tests as learning rather than failure. If a new creative concept underperforms the control, that's useful information — it tells you something about your audience that you can act on in the next brief. Teams that punish experimental failure create environments where people stop experimenting and start playing it safe. You end up optimizing the same campaign indefinitely instead of discovering what the next step-change looks like.

## Democratizing Data Across the Team

A data-driven mindset doesn't scale when it lives in one or two people on the team. If the only person who can pull a campaign report is the analyst, decisions either wait for the analyst or happen without data. Neither is a good system, and in a fast-moving campaign environment, the bottleneck compounds quickly.

Building data literacy across a marketing team isn't about turning everyone into a data scientist. It's about raising the floor. Most marketers can learn to read a basic analytics dashboard, understand what statistical significance means, build a pivot table, and identify when a number looks suspicious. Those skills don't require a quantitative background — they require curiosity and a bit of deliberate practice over time.

> Data literacy isn't a technical credential. It's the habit of asking for evidence before forming an opinion, and being willing to update that opinion when the evidence changes.

## My Own Evolution

My evolution toward a data-driven mindset happened through a series of small moments rather than a single revelation. It was the first time a metric I was tracking moved in a direction I didn't expect, and I had to go back and figure out why — and discovered that the answer changed what the team should do. It was building a tracker that the team actually used and realizing that clear presentation of data changes how people think and communicate in a room together.

It was presenting a recommendation backed by six weeks of brand health data and having it taken more seriously than a creative pitch without that foundation. That moment was instructive. Not because the data made me right, but because it changed the quality of the conversation — it gave the team something to examine and stress-test rather than something to either accept or reject on instinct.

None of those moments were about technical sophistication. They were about the discipline of making evidence central to every marketing question and every recommendation. That's the mindset shift. The tools — Excel, Power BI, whatever comes next — are just how you put it into practice. The mindset has to come first.

## A Continuous Practice

Building a data-driven mindset isn't a destination you arrive at and then stop working on. It's a continuous practice of intellectual honesty — of choosing, repeatedly and deliberately, to look for evidence before forming an opinion, and to update your beliefs when the evidence requires it. That practice is harder on some days than others. It's harder when you're under time pressure, when the data is ambiguous, or when the evidence contradicts something you believed in.

But it compounds. Every decision made on evidence rather than assumption builds a slightly more reliable track record. Every test run and learned from builds a slightly richer understanding of how your consumer responds. Over time, the gap between the evidence-based marketer and the instinct-only marketer becomes impossible to ignore. I'm at the early stages of that compounding curve, and I intend to stay on it.
